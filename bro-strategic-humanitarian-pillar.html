Military Infrastructure

BRO: A Strategic and Humanitarian Pillar of Border Empowerment

Trilok Nath & Priyanka Bisht · 2025

Introduction

Behind every soldier standing guard at India's borders lies an equally vital support system — the roads, bridges, and lifelines that connect them to the farthest frontiers. The Border Roads Organisation (BRO), established on 7 May 1960, began with Projects Tusker (Vartak) and Beacon and has since grown into 18 projects spanning 11 states, three Union Territories, and Bhutan. Blending military discipline with technical expertise, BRO doesn't just build roads — it builds resilience, carving paths through snow-covered mountains, rushing aid during disasters, and connecting forgotten villages to the national mainstream. This paper examines BRO as far more than a road-builder: it is the silent bridge between India's defence and its remotest communities.

Historical Background

Founded under Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru with the motto "Shramena Sarvam Sadhyam" ("any goal can be achieved through hard work"), BRO was created to deliver connectivity to India's most inaccessible border regions so the armed forces could reach and defend them. Over six decades, its mandate expanded from pure road-building to constructing tunnels, airfields, and other strategic infrastructure — work that extends beyond India's borders into neighbouring countries, reinforcing diplomatic ties alongside national security.

Nation-Building and BRO's Role

Nation-building for a country as vast and diverse as India means physically connecting its remotest regions to the core. Roads and tunnels are not just structures — they are how the state makes its presence felt in border areas, instilling security and belonging among local communities. BRO strengthens defence mobility while simultaneously connecting isolated populations to healthcare, education, and markets, generating local employment and curbing distress migration. Projects like the Atal Tunnel (all-weather access to Leh-Ladakh), the Umling La Road (the world's highest motorable pass), and the Char Dham Project (linking religious, cultural, and economic development) exemplify how BRO's infrastructure sustains unity and long-term stability.

Organisational Structure

BRO is led by the Director General Border Roads (DGBR), supported by the Integrated Financial Advisor and Principal Controller of Defence Accounts for financial oversight, and by Additional Director Generals overseeing regional zones (North West, East). Functional directorates under the DGBR handle technical planning, personnel, development and vigilance, medical services, budget and training, and legal affairs — together forming a coordinated structure equipped to meet India's border infrastructure needs.

Capital Allocation (FY 2020–21 to 2025–26)

BRO's budget has risen steadily: from ₹2,500 crore in 2021–22 to ₹3,500 crore (2022–23, +40%), ₹5,000 crore (2023–24, +43%), ₹6,500 crore (2024–25), and ₹7,146.5 crore in 2025–26 (+9.74%) — reflecting the scale-up needed for tunnels and bridges on major river gaps. (Source: PIB, India Today, Swarajya, PRS Legislative Research)

Empowering Women in BRO

Long a male-dominated organisation given its terrain and climate, BRO began actively inducting women into field roles after its 2021 empowerment agenda. Milestones include Vaishali S. Hiwase (2021), the first woman to command a Road Construction Company; Major Aaina Rana (2021), who led India's first all-women RCC building the road to Mana Pass; Col. Navneet Duggal, the first woman EME officer to command a field workshop; and Col. Ponung Doming (2023), who led the task force constructing the world's highest motorable road. These appointments mark a genuine shift in BRO's culture alongside its security mission.

Act East Policy and Broader Importance

BRO underpins India's Act East policy through projects like the Imphal–Moreh road, strengthening trade and cooperation with Southeast Asia. More broadly, BRO's importance spans the strategic (rapid troop and logistics movement), economic (connecting remote regions to trade and tourism), humanitarian (disaster relief), and social (local employment and national integration) domains.

Humanitarian Role

Operating in disaster-prone terrain, BRO routinely acts as first responder — restoring roads after the 2013 Kedarnath floods, the 2021 Chamoli glacier burst, the 2023 Sikkim flash floods, and the 2025 Dharali cloudburst in Uttarkashi, ensuring relief material and rescue teams reach affected populations without delay.

Challenges

BRO faces recurring hurdles: unpredictable terrain and a narrow construction window in the fragile, young Himalayan range; chronic manpower shortages given the harsh, isolating nature of the work; administrative and bureaucratic delays that slow project completion; and the growing threat of climate change to existing infrastructure, requiring modern, climate-resilient construction methods.

Conclusion

BRO remains an often-invisible but indispensable pillar of India's border security and nation-building project — providing lifelines through some of the world's harshest terrain while advancing socio-economic inclusion, women's empowerment, and disaster response. As geopolitical and climate challenges intensify, BRO's role — including its infrastructure work in Bhutan, Myanmar, and Afghanistan — will only grow in strengthening India's borders and its Neighbourhood First policy.
